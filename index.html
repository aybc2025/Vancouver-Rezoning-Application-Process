Community Plans and Policies

Our policy planners write plans and policies that outline future community objectives.

What They Do:

  • Set out the long-term vision and future growth for a community, including its amenities
  • Guide how land can be developed through zoning, including land uses and what can be built
  • Outline where we may consider a rezoning in response to City Council priorities

Good to Know

After engaging with the community, policy planners present community plans and policies to Council to decide on.

Timeline: Varies - community plans are developed over months or years

Step 1: Pre-application and Enquiry

Our rezoning planners provide detailed advice to potential applicants on early rezoning proposals.

What Happens:

  • Some applicants submit a preliminary rezoning proposal for review before formally applying
  • All reviews of proposals are informed by Council-approved plans and policies
  • Rezoning planners respond to the applicant's enquiry
  • Planners may ask the applicant to hold a pre-application open house to show early design ideas

Your Voice Matters Here

Pre-application Open House (if held): The applicant invites the community to the open house. Your feedback will help inform their next steps.

Note: Pre-application open houses are not listed on the City's website. The applicant invites the community directly.

What you can do:

  • Attend the open house if the applicant holds one
  • Ask questions about the proposal
  • Share your concerns and ideas
  • Connect with neighbors

Requirements for Developers

When to Submit an Enquiry:

  • Required if your proposal is NOT consistent with an ODP
  • Required if it's a significant departure from policy
  • Recommended for all major projects

What to Include:

  • Site plans and conceptual designs
  • Explanation of how the proposal aligns with or departs from policy
  • Preliminary shadow studies and impact assessments

Pre-application Open House:

  • May be required by staff before formal application
  • You must invite the community directly
  • Document all feedback received
  • Not listed on City website - this is your responsibility

Important

Staff advice does NOT guarantee approval. It reflects current policy at the time of enquiry.

Typical Duration: 1-3 months

Step 2: Application

Our rezoning planners review formal rezoning applications in detail and seek public input.

The Process:

  • The applicant submits a formal rezoning application informed by early advice and Council-approved plans
  • Rezoning planners notify the public with a project site sign, postcard, and website listing
  • For most applications, planners hold an online Q&A period to share information and get input
  • Feedback may also be sought from citizen-led advisory committees

Your Voice Matters Here - This is Key!

How to Participate:

  1. Online Q&A Period: Ask questions and provide feedback during the online engagement period
  2. Shape Your City: Visit the project page to learn more and submit comments
  3. Written Feedback: Email your comments directly to the rezoning team
  4. Talk to Neighbors: Organize with your community to submit collective feedback

What Happens to Your Feedback:

  • All comments are read by rezoning staff
  • Feedback is summarized in the staff report to Council
  • Your input helps shape the recommendation
  • Comments may lead to design changes

Stay Informed

You'll be notified by:

  • Site sign on the property (with QR code)
  • Postcard to your address (if nearby)
  • Shape Your City updates (if subscribed)

Application Requirements

Documents You Must Submit:

  • Completed application form
  • Site plans, floor plans, elevations
  • Shadow studies
  • Transportation impact assessment
  • Arborist report (if applicable)
  • Heritage assessment (if applicable)
  • Environmental reports as required

Application Fees:

  • Base fee varies by project size and complexity
  • Additional fees for enhanced rezoning proposals
  • See Zoning and Development Fee By-law for current rates

Timeline Expectations

Processing Time: 3-6 months typically

  • Clock starts when application is deemed "complete"
  • Incomplete applications delay the process
  • Respond promptly to staff requests for information
  • Applications inactive for 6+ months may be cancelled

Public Consultation - Your Responsibility

Be prepared to:

  • Respond to public questions during Q&A period
  • Address concerns raised by the community
  • Make design changes based on feedback when appropriate
  • Document all engagement activities

Find a Rezoning Application

Search for active rezoning applications on Shape Your City or the City's rezoning website.

Typical Duration: 3-6 months

Step 3: Report Writing

Our staff make a recommendation to City Council about rezoning applications.

A: Report Writing

Rezoning planners write a report for City Council that:

  • Describes the application
  • Summarizes all the feedback received
  • Recommends to refer the application to a public hearing or Council meeting (if applicable)
  • Recommends whether to approve or refuse the application

B: Pathways to Council

Fast Track Path

For applications that are:

  • Consistent with an Official Development Plan (ODP)
  • Include significant residential use (50%+ of floor area)

Result: Council meeting scheduled - no public hearing required.

Optional Path

For applications that are:

  • Consistent with an ODP
  • Less than 50% residential (majority industrial, office, or institutional)

Result: Council decides: public hearing OR Council meeting.

Full Process Path

For applications that are:

  • NOT consistent with an ODP

Result: Public hearing required.

C: Referral to Public Hearing

For referral reports, rezoning planners work with City Clerks to add the report to a Council meeting agenda. At this meeting, Council decides whether to refer the application to a future public hearing or Council meeting.

Important Note

During the referral step, Council must not discuss the rezoning application with the applicant or the public. This ensures Council doesn't receive information that the public and applicant don't have before deciding.

Typical Duration: 2-4 months

Step 4: Council Decision

City Council must consider and decide on all rezoning applications (all zoning by-law changes) and heritage designations, according to the Vancouver Charter.

Notification:

Rezoning planners notify the public with:

  • Project site sign
  • Postcard to neighbors
  • Website listing

Meeting coordinators usually publish the meeting agenda one week before the meeting.

At the Public Hearing or Council Meeting:

Council's role is to listen to the public when deciding on a rezoning application. For each agenda item:

  1. The rezoning application is summarized along with correspondence (public feedback) received
  2. Rezoning planners present the application and recommendation to Council
  3. The applicant may also present to Council
  4. If a public hearing, registered members of the public speak to Council
  5. Council discusses the application
  6. The mayor calls for a vote on the motion to approve, amend, or refuse the application

Your Voice Matters Here - Final Opportunity!

Two Ways to Give Feedback:

  1. Send comments or petition online:
    • Submit through the City's website before the meeting
    • Your name and comments will be published in the meeting agenda
    • Council reads all written submissions
    • You can submit individually or as a group
  2. Request to speak at the meeting (if Public Hearing):
    • Register to speak in advance
    • Usually get 5 minutes to speak
    • Your name and comments will be in meeting records
    • Speak clearly about how the project affects you

Tips for Effective Input:

  • Be specific about your concerns
  • Reference policy and community plans when possible
  • Suggest specific changes or conditions
  • Stay respectful and factual
  • Focus on land use issues (not personal attacks)

What Council Considers

Council weighs:

  • Alignment with community plans and policies
  • Public feedback (yours!)
  • Staff recommendations
  • Community amenity contributions
  • Impacts on the neighborhood

Presenting to Council

Preparation is Key:

  • You may present your application to Council (usually 10-15 minutes)
  • Prepare clear, concise slides showing key benefits
  • Address concerns raised in public feedback
  • Highlight how you've responded to community input
  • Be ready to answer Council's questions

What to Emphasize:

  • Alignment with ODP and policy
  • Community benefits and amenities
  • How you addressed feedback
  • Economic and social benefits
  • Quality of design and sustainability

Possible Outcomes

Council may:

  • Approve: Proceed to conditions and enactment
  • Approve with amendments: Changes to conditions or design
  • Refuse: Application denied
  • Refer back: Request more information or changes

If approved: You'll receive a list of conditions to fulfill before enactment.

Timeline Planning

  • Meetings are scheduled weeks in advance
  • Public hearings can run late into the evening
  • Be patient - multiple applications may be heard
  • Decision is usually made the same night

Stay Informed

View Council meetings and agendas, get notified about upcoming meetings, and follow along on social media.

Meeting Duration: Varies - public hearings can last several hours

Step 5: Zoning Enactment

City Council enacts a by-law to change the zoning.

What Needs to Happen:

  • The applicant needs to meet all legal conditions of rezoning
  • This includes paying any required community amenity contributions
  • This work usually happens in the months after rezoning applications are approved

Final Steps:

When all conditions are met:

  1. City lawyers liaise with rezoning planners and meeting coordinators to confirm enactment
  2. The zoning by-law is added to a Council meeting agenda
  3. Council votes to enact the zoning by-law as the final step in rezoning

What This Means for You

After Council approval:

  • The developer must fulfill all conditions before construction can begin
  • This can take 3-12 months depending on complexity
  • Community amenity contributions are secured at this stage
  • Once enacted, the rezoning is final and construction can proceed

Tracking Progress:

  • You can check the project status on Shape Your City
  • Development permit applications are publicly posted
  • Building permit applications are also public

Conditions to Fulfill

Common Conditions Include:

  • Community Amenity Contribution (CAC): Payment or in-kind contribution
  • Development Cost Levies (DCLs): Infrastructure fees
  • Legal agreements: Housing agreements, heritage agreements, etc.
  • Servicing agreements: Water, sewer, streets
  • Dedications: Lane dedications, park land, etc.
  • Bonds and securities: Landscape bonds, etc.

Financial Requirements

Be Prepared to Pay:

  • Community amenity contributions (negotiated during rezoning)
  • Development cost levies (set by by-law)
  • Utility connection fees
  • Legal and registration costs

Timing: All payments must be made before enactment

Legal Process

Work with City Lawyers to:

  • Draft and execute legal agreements
  • Register covenants and easements
  • Complete land title work
  • Obtain necessary approvals from other agencies

Timeline: This process typically takes 3-12 months

Managing the Timeline

Tips to Avoid Delays:

  • Start working on conditions immediately after approval
  • Maintain regular communication with City staff
  • Hire qualified professionals (lawyers, engineers)
  • Don't wait until the last minute to address complex issues
  • Be prepared for unexpected requirements

Completion

Once the zoning by-law is enacted, the rezoning process is complete. The applicant can then apply for development and building permits.

Typical Duration: 3-12 months (depends on conditions)

Development and Building Permit

Our permit coordinators review permit applications and issue development and building permits.

Development Permit:

After a rezoning application is approved, the applicant can apply for a development permit.

  • Development permits ensure the detailed building design complies with the Zoning and Development By-law
  • The by-law regulates: land uses, building location, maximum height and size, and other provisions necessary for good city building
  • The design is informed by conditions of approval from the rezoning
  • Permit coordinators issue the permit once all conditions are met

Building Permit:

Once the development permit is issued, the applicant can apply for a building permit to start construction.

  • Building permits ensure the building complies with the Vancouver Building By-law
  • This by-law regulates life safety, liveability, accessibility, and sustainability

Find a Development Permit Application

Search for active development permit applications on the City's website.

Typical Duration: 3-6 months for development permit, 2-4 months for building permit

Find Your Pathway

Answer a few questions to understand which process path applies to your situation.

Is your proposal consistent with an Official Development Plan (ODP)?

An ODP is a Council-adopted plan that outlines land use, density, and building form for a specific area.

Is more than 50% of the gross floor area residential?

Calculate the total proposed floor area dedicated to residential use (apartments, condos, townhouses) versus other uses (commercial, office, industrial).

Fast Track Path

Your proposal qualifies for the streamlined process:

  • No public hearing required
  • Decision at Council meeting
  • Typically faster approval timeline

What This Means:

Since your proposal is consistent with the ODP and is primarily residential, Council will consider it at a Council meeting without a public hearing. However, there will still be opportunities for public input during the application stage.

Optional Path

Your proposal may follow either route:

  • Council will decide: public hearing OR Council meeting
  • Timeline depends on Council's decision

What This Means:

Your proposal is consistent with the ODP but is less than 50% residential. Council has the option to refer it to a public hearing or proceed directly to a Council meeting for decision. You'll be notified which path is chosen.

Full Process Path

Your proposal requires a public hearing:

  • Public hearing required
  • Full public participation opportunities
  • Longer approval timeline

What This Means:

Since your proposal is not consistent with the ODP, it must go through a public hearing where community members can speak directly to Council. This ensures thorough public input for proposals that vary from adopted plans.

Glossary

Official Development Plan (ODP)

A Council-adopted plan that guides development in a specific area. It outlines land use, density, building form, and community amenities for that area.

Rezoning

The process of changing the zoning designation of a property to allow for different types or scales of development than currently permitted.

Development Approval Procedure (DAP) By-law

A by-law that establishes procedures for rezoning and ODP amendment applications. Effective June 9, 2025.

Public Hearing

A formal meeting where Council hears from the public before making a decision on a rezoning application. Required for applications not consistent with an ODP.

Council Meeting

A regular meeting of City Council where decisions are made on various City matters, including some rezoning applications.

Development Permit

A permit that ensures a building's detailed design complies with zoning regulations. Required before construction can begin.

Building Permit

A permit that ensures a building meets safety, accessibility, and sustainability standards. Required to start construction.

Rezoning Enquiry

A formal preliminary proposal submitted before a full application. Required for proposals that significantly depart from policy or an ODP.

Community Amenity Contribution (CAC)

Financial or in-kind contributions made by developers to help fund community amenities and infrastructure as a condition of rezoning.

Zoning and Development By-law

The by-law that regulates land use, building height, density, and other development standards across Vancouver.

Shape Your City

The City's online engagement platform where you can view active rezoning applications, ask questions, and provide feedback.

Applicant

The property owner or their authorized representative who submits a rezoning application.

Zoning Enactment

The final step where Council formally passes the by-law to change the zoning, after all conditions have been met.
